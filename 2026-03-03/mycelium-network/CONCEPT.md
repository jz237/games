# Mycelium Network

## Premise
You are a fungal colony growing through a petri dish. Spread tendrils outward to consume nutrient nodes before rival colonies do. But here's the twist: tendrils cost energy to maintain, and you can starve your own network by overextending. It's a real-time strategy game about knowing when to grow fast and when to consolidate.

## Visual Style
**Bioluminescent microscopy aesthetic.** The petri dish is dark, almost black. Fungal threads glow in cold white-green-blue, pulsing softly as nutrients flow through them. Nutrient nodes look like glowing amber spores. Rival colonies glow in contrasting hot colors — orange, red. The entire thing looks like a science documentary shot at 40× magnification.

- Background: deep charcoal with subtle agar texture rings
- Your colony: cool cyan-green bioluminescent threads, faint pulse animation
- Rival: warm orange-red threads
- Nutrients: golden amber orbs with glow halo
- Dead tendrils: fade to grey-brown, curl inward and disappear
- UI: minimal — energy bar shown as glow intensity of your colony

## Core Mechanics
1. **Growth** — Click/tap any existing tendril tip to extend. New segments cost energy
2. **Nutrient absorption** — Connect a tip to a node. Energy flows back through the network
3. **Tendril fatigue** — Long isolated branches slowly drain energy. Must be fed or they die
4. **Collision** — Two colonies meeting at a node fight: larger energy reserve wins, loser branch dies
5. **Spore burst** — Spend big energy to send a spore across the dish (long jump, high cost)
6. **Network efficiency** — Shorter path to nutrients = faster energy return. Dead branches litter the dish

## What Makes It Weird
The game is played at the speed of thought — no turns, pure real-time. But the scale is geological: growing one tendril takes 4–5 seconds of real time. It's a game that rewards patience and long-term planning in 90-second rounds. Watching two AI colonies compete is hypnotic — like watching time-lapse footage of something alive and hungry.
